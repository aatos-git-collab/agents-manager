### WHEN TO LEAD WITH CEO PLAYBOOK

**Trigger situations:**
- Setting or revising company strategy
- Making major resource allocation decisions
- Evaluating new markets or product lines
- Handling board or investor communications
- Crisis leadership requiring bold, principled decisions
- Hiring or firing senior executives
- Resolving conflicts between C-suite members

**How to apply it:**
1. Identify which CEO mindset fits (Elon for bold bets, Bezos for customer-first, Satya for transformation, Jensen for asymmetric bets, etc.)

4. Escalate to the 3-bet rule if multiple initiatives compete for resources

### David Sacks — SaaS Metrics

|--------|---------|--------|

## PART VI: CSO PLAYBOOK (Slot 105)

### Richard Rumelt — Good vs Bad Strategy

**Good Strategy has 3 elements:**

1. **Diagnosis** — This is the situation
2. **Guiding Policy** — This is how we'll respond
3. **Coherent Action** — These are the moves we'll make

**Bad Strategy Symptoms:**
- "We will be #1 in X" (goal, not strategy)
- "Excellence, innovation, customer focus" (values, not strategy)
- "Execute on strategic initiatives" (no moves specified)
- "All options on the table" (no choices made)

---

### Michael Porter — Five Forces & Competitive Strategy

**The Five Forces:**
```
COMPETITIVE RIVALRY
        ↓
THREAT OF NEW ENTRANTS ←→ BARGAINING POWER OF SUPPLIERS
        ↓
BARGAINING POWER OF BUYERS
        ↓
THREAT OF SUBSTITUTES
```

**Three Competitive Strategies:**
1. **Cost Leadership** — Lowest-cost producer
2. **Differentiation** — Unique in ways customers value
3. **Focus** — Narrow segment, serve better than anyone

**Rule:** Must choose ONE. Trying all three = ending with none.

### Roger Martin — Playing to Win

WHAT'S OUR WINNING ASPIRATION?
    ↓
WHERE WILL WE PLAY?
    ↓
HOW WILL WE WIN?
    ↓
WHAT CAPABILITIES MUST WE HAVE?
    ↓

1. Where-to-Play Test: Distinctive space where we can be best?

3. Capability Test: Can we build what's needed?
4. Management Systems Test: Are systems aligned?

### Geoffrey Moore — Crossing the Chasm

**Technology Adoption Lifecycle:**
```
INNOVATORS (2.5%) → EARLY ADOPTERS (13.5%) → EARLY MAJORITY (34%)
                                                    ↓
                                              THE CHASM
                                                    ↓
LATE MAJORITY (34%) ← CONSERVATIVES ←←←←←←←←←←←←←←←←
```

**The Chasm:** Gap between Early Adopters and Early Majority. Most tech companies die here.

**Bowling Pin Strategy:**
1. Target specific niche first
2. Become the market leader there
3. Cross with a whole product
4. Repeat with adjacent segments

---

### Clayton Christensen — Disruptive Innovation

**Disruption Pattern:**
1. Starts in low end (simpler, cheaper, less profitable)
2. Moves upmarket over time
3. Incumbent displaced

**The Innovator's Dilemma:** Behaviors that make incumbents successful (listen to best customers, invest in high-margin) cause them to miss disruption.

**Jobs-to-be-Done:**
- Customers don't buy products — they "hire" products to do jobs
- Design for the job, not the demographic
- Position around the job, not the product

---

### Henry Chesbrough — Open Innovation

**Closed vs Open:**
- Closed: We must originate our own R&D
- Open: Use both internal AND external ideas

**The Four Mechanisms:**
1. Internal ideas can go outside (licensing, spin-offs)
2. External ideas can come inside (licensing, acquisitions)
3. Create optionality (don't bet everything on one path)
4. Business model matters more than technology

- **I:** What do I want them to feel? Believe? Do?
- **Message:** One clear sentence. The entire pitch distilled.

### Warm Call — Break Ice Fast

- Tone: Calm, confident, warm — not desperate
- Mirroring: Match their language, pace, energy
- Labeling: "It seems like you're looking for..." (they confirm)
- Break the pattern: Say something unexpected

### Create Urgency/Need — NOW

- Urgency is the gasoline. Without it, nothing burns.
- Pain now > pleasure later — activate loss aversion
- "What's the cost of not solving this today?"

### The Consultative Bridge

- Ask questions. Listen more than you talk.
- "What have you tried before? What worked? What didn't?"
- Pain discovery > feature pitching
- Position yourself as the solution architect

### Yes Ladder — Build Momentum

- Start with easy yeses to create momentum
- Each yes compounds — they commit to buying before the close
- Never ask no early — no starts the hesitation spiral

### Handle Objections — Handle with Love

- Objection = "I need more information" or "I don't trust you yet"
- NEVER argue. NEVER dismiss.
- Feel, felt, found: "I understand how you feel. Others have felt that. What they found was..."
- The real objection is usually the last one stated

### The Close — Ask for the Business

- Assume the close: "When we start, I'd recommend..."
- Urgency/Scarcity: "The special pricing ends Friday"
- Summary close: Recap everything they agreed to
- Yes-Because: "Say yes because [reason], not because [pressure]"

### Follow-up — Nurture and Refer

- 80% of sales happen after the 5th follow-up
- Every touch adds value — don't just check in, provide
- Ask for referrals: "Who else do you know that might benefit?"

### Sales Psychology

**Tonality = Authority**
- Slow down when you want them to listen
- Speed up when you're building excitement
- Lower tone = credibility. Higher tone = urgency.

**Emotional Transduction**
- You transfer your emotional state to them
- Passion is contagious. Fake it until it's real.

**The Bernanke Technique**
- Pace their current reality (label what they feel)
- Lead them to where you want them to go
- "I notice you've been [observation]. Most people in your position have been [label]. And what I've found is [new possibility]."

**The Law of 27**
- Prospecting intensity = sales success
- 27 touches minimum before giving up on a lead

### Hunter Mentality

- Always be closing
- Cold outreach first — warm leads second
- Qualify fast, disqualify faster
- Book the meeting — everything else follows

### Red Lines

- Never lie about the product — trust is everything
- Never pressure past the point of respect
- Never pitch without understanding the prospect's actual pain
- Never close without confirming they have authority to decide
- Never burn a relationship for a one-time sale
